## Learning goal

Explain **bounded-cache** strategies that drop tokens on purpose—windows, attention sinks, and heavy hitters—and state the **quality risk** honestly.

## Builds on lessons 2–3

- Attention mass is uneven (lesson 2).
- Full history KV grows without bound in `S` (lesson 3).

## Why forget?

For very long streams, keeping every past token’s K/V is impossible. Systems **evict** entries under a budget. That is **lossy** relative to full context: “stream forever” ≠ “remember everything perfectly.”

## Policy family (teaching)

| Policy             | Keep                                      | Drop intuition   | Anchors            |
| ------------------ | ----------------------------------------- | ---------------- | ------------------ |
| **Window**         | Last `W` tokens                           | Everything older | Sliding window     |
| **Sinks + window** | First `K` + last `W`                      | Middle mass      | StreamingLLM `S19` |
| **Heavy + window** | High cumulative-attention tokens + recent | Low-score middle | H₂O flavor `S18`   |
| **H₂O-style mix**  | Sinks + heavies + recent under one budget | Rest             | Combined intuition |

SnapKV-style observation windows and other sparsifiers exist; we keep the lab to this family so tradeoffs stay visible.

## What the scene shows

- Token stream: **bright = kept**, **dim = evicted** under the selected policy + **Eviction budget**.
- Attention surface: sinks (●) and heavy hitters (■) as in lesson 2—now tied to _why_ some indices survive.

Metrics do not invent perplexity. We only show **how many tokens left the keep-set** (capacity story). Quality impact is **task- and model-dependent**—quote papers carefully.

<TryThis>

- Set policy to **window** with a small budget: early tokens go dim—even if they were sinks.
- Switch to **sinks + window**: early tokens return.
- Try **h2o-style mix**: sinks and mid “heavies” compete with recency under the same budget.
- Raise **Eviction budget**: fewer dim beads.

</TryThis>
