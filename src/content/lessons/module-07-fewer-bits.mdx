## Learning goal

Show how **lower bit-width KV payloads** shrink memory, why savings are not magical perfect divisions, and why **keys and values** may need different treatment.

## Builds on lesson 3

`M_KV` scales with **bytes per element** (`bpe`). If the payload uses fewer bits, the leading term drops—before counting metadata.

## Scaling the formula

<Formula>
{`M_KV(bpe′) ≈ M_KV(bpe) · (bpe′ / bpe)

Examples (payload only, metadata ignored):
FP16 bpe = 2
FP8 bpe = 1 → ~2× smaller payload
INT4 bpe = 0.5 → ~4× smaller payload
INT2 bpe = 0.25 → ~8× smaller payload`}

</Formula>

Real schemes also store **scales**, **zero-points**, **group indices**, and often a **residual window** of full-precision recent tokens (`S07` KIVI). Teach payload scaling as a **lower bound** on savings, then add overhead.

## Why “just quantize everything the same way” fails

- **Keys** and **values** can have different outlier structure; research often quantizes them **asymmetrically** (e.g. KIVI: K per-channel, V per-token) (`S07`).
- Aggressive low-bit KV can hurt quality; claims need method + model + residual policy—not a single perplexity number invented here.

## Orthogonal idea: weight quant vs KV quant

Quantizing **weights** (GPTQ, AWQ, …) is related but separate from quantizing the **cache**. Production stacks often combine both; this lesson isolates the cache term.
